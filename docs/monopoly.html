8.1 Monopoly

A monopoly is an an industry that has only one producer of the product and there no close substitutes. We will see that a monopolist tends to reduce output in order to raise price. However, to increase the quantity sold, a monopolist must lower the price on each unit sold. The monopolist makes money on the additional quantity sold and loses money on the goods that it was already selling, as these are now sold at a lower price. Reasons for a monopoly to exit are usually barriers to entry.

  • Economies of scale: One firm can operate at lower average cost than multiple firms and there are decreasing average cost over the entire market range. A natural monopoly can produce for the entire market and/or there may be large sunk cost and/or fixed cost.
  • Legal barriers: Protection of intellectual property (e.g., copyrights, patents), control over a scarce resource, or barriers put in place by firms to discourage entry.
  • Network externalities: Added benefits for all users of a good or service that arise because other people are using it too. Joining a large network is more beneficial than joining a small network. Think about Facebook and LinkedIn.

As opposed to perfect competition, there is the possibility of profits in the long-run. Examples of monopolies are Microsoft (to a certain extent), the IUPUI parking system, or De Beers. Groups can also act as a monopolist (cartel) such as OPEC, unions, or United Potato Growers of America. Note that a monopoly can create competition for itself in the case of durable goods monopolies. A durable good does not depreciate once it is on the market. Imagine that you are the owner of the only gold mine on Earth. At the beginning, you have a monopoly. But assuming that gold is never transformed into something else (e.g., like gasoline is burned), there will soon be a second hand gold market which competes with your monopoly. Another example of a durable monopoly that you can all relate to are textbooks.

8.1.1 Monopoly Output: Overview

The monopolist will set marginal revenue equal to marginal cost, i.e., \(MR=MC\), in order to find the profit maximizing output quantity. Recall that under perfect competition, we have marginal cost pricing, i.e., \(p=MC\). It is important to understand that firm in a perfectly competitive market also sets \(MR=MC\) but because the price is determined by the market, the marginal revenue from selling an additional unit is \(p\) (Figure ). The following two example illustrate the monopolist’s output decision under (1) constant and (2) increasing marginal cost. Note that a monopolistic market results in a deadweight loss. This is not caused by higher prices for consumers of higher profits for the monopolist but by the underproduction of goods.

8.1.2 Monopoly Output: Constant Marginal Cost

Assume a monopolist is faced with a demand that can be written as \(Q=50-P/2\). The inverse demand function can thus be written as \(P=100-2 \cdot Q\). The revenue can be written as \[R = P \cdot Q = (100-2 \cdot Q ) \cdot Q = 100 \cdot Q - 2 \cdot Q^2\] Marginal revenue is written as \(MR = 100 - 4 \cdot Q\). Assume \(MC =20\), then under perfect competition, we have \(MC = P\), i.e., \(20 = 100-2 \cdot Q\). And hence, the socially optimum output quantity is \(Q_c=20\). Under monopoly pricing, we have \(MC = MR\), i.e., \(20 = 100-4 \cdot Q\). And hence, the monopoly output quantity is \(Q_m=20\). This situation is illustrated in Panel (a) of the figure below.

8.1.3 Monopoly Output: Increasing Marginal Cost}

Suppose that the cost function is written as \(C = 100 + Q^2\) with increasing marginal cost function \(MC = 2 \cdot Q\). If the inverse demand function is written as \(P=100-2 \cdot Q\), then the marginal revenue function is \(MR = 100 - 4 \cdot Q\). Similar to the example with constant marginal cost, we set \(MR=MC\), i.e., \[100 - 4 \cdot Q = 2 \cdot Q\] Solving leads to \(Q_m=100/6\) which is illustrated in Panel (b), Figure . What is the socially optimum output? Assume the government wants to achieve the socially optimal outcome with regulation. Further, assume that the government envisions taxing the monopolist. Does this solve the problem?

A monopolist produces on the elastic portion of the demand curve. It can be shown that \[\underbrace{\frac{p-MC}{p}}_{\text{Lerner Index}} = -\frac{1}{\epsilon_D}\] The interpretation of the above equation is that the more elastic demand the higher the availability of substitutes. Markup over marginal cost is reduced if the elasticity becomes larger (Figure ). Suppose a profit-maximizing monopolist is producing 800 units of output and is charging a price of $40 per unit. If the price elasticity of demand for the product is -2, find the marginal cost of the last unit produced. Answer: $20 \[\frac{p-MC}{p} = -\frac{1}{\epsilon_D} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad p \left(1+\frac{1}{\epsilon_D} \right) = MC\]
